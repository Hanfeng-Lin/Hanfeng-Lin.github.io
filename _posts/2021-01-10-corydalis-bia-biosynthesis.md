I'm excited to share my co-first-author paper, "Integration of Full-Length Transcriptomics and Targeted Metabolomics to Identify Benzylisoquinoline Alkaloid Biosynthetic Genes in *Corydalis yanhusuo*", published in *Horticulture Research*!

This was my first published research paper, completed during my undergraduate years at China Pharmaceutical University — and it holds a special place for me as the project where I first learned to think like a scientist.

---

**Why *Corydalis yanhusuo*?**

*Corydalis yanhusuo* (延胡索, Yuan-Hu) is a classic herb in traditional Chinese medicine, used for thousands of years to relieve pain and improve blood circulation. Its therapeutic effects come from benzylisoquinoline alkaloids (BIAs) — a structurally diverse family of plant secondary metabolites that includes well-known compounds like tetrahydropalmatine and tetrahydroberberine.

Despite the long history of medicinal use, the biosynthetic genes responsible for making these alkaloids in *C. yanhusuo* were poorly characterized. Earlier transcriptomic studies relied on short-read sequencing, which produces fragmented transcripts that are hard to assemble into complete gene sequences — leaving many biosynthetic steps unresolved.

---

**The Approach: Hybrid Sequencing**

We combined **PacBio long-read sequencing** (which captures full-length transcripts) with **Illumina short-read sequencing** (for error correction) to obtain the first full-length transcriptome of *C. yanhusuo*. This hybrid approach gave us 90 full-length transcripts covering the entire known BIA biosynthetic pathway.

Paired with targeted metabolomics, we confirmed that tetrahydroberberine and tetrahydropalmatine — the two major BIAs — accumulate preferentially in the roots, consistent with the tissue-specific expression patterns of the identified biosynthetic genes.

---

**A Key Finding: The Final Methylation Step**

One of the most exciting results was our phylogenetic analysis of O-methyltransferase (OMT) enzymes. The final step in tetrahydropalmatine synthesis requires a specific columbamine-O-methyltransferase activity. We identified **ten candidate enzymes** that may carry out this reaction — a finding that opens the door for functional validation and potential metabolic engineering of this valuable compound.
